# RequiredFilings.com — Content Principles

---

## The One Rule That Governs Everything

**Write for the business owner who knows nothing about compliance, not for the CA who knows everything.**

Every word on this site should make a first-time reader say "I understand exactly what this is and why I need it." If it makes them feel stupid, it fails. If it makes us sound clever, it fails.

---

## Who We Are Writing For

A business owner in India who:

- Is starting, running, or scaling a business
- Has no background in law, tax, or compliance
- Is time-poor and decision-fatigued
- Trusts plain clarity over professional jargon
- Needs to know what to do, not how the system works

Write every sentence with this person in mind.

---

## Content Principles

---

### 01. Clarity Over Expertise

Never write to impress. Write to inform.

If a sentence requires the reader to already know something to understand it, rewrite it. The goal is zero confusion, not maximum credibility. Expertise shows through clarity, not complexity.

**Wrong:** "We facilitate end-to-end statutory compliance for corporate entities under the Companies Act, 2013."

**Right:** "We register your company and keep it legally compliant — from day one."

---

### 02. Lead With What the Reader Gets

Every page, every section, every paragraph opens with the outcome — not the process, not the feature, not the service name.

Readers do not care what the service is called. They care what changes for them after they use it.

**Wrong:** "GST Returns Filing involves monthly or quarterly submission of GSTR forms to the GST portal."

**Right:** "Your GST returns are filed correctly and on time. No penalties. No notices."

---

### 03. One Idea Per Sentence

Each sentence carries one thought. No compound ideas. No stacking clauses.

This is not a readability trick. It is a respect for the reader's time. When sentences are short and single-minded, the reader moves fast and retains more.

Maximum sentence length: 12 words.
Maximum paragraph length: 3 sentences.

---

### 04. No Jargon Without an Immediate Plain-English Follow-Up

Some jargon is unavoidable — GST, ROC, LLP, IEC. These are the actual names of the things.

The rule: every time a technical term appears for the first time on a page, the next sentence explains it in plain English.

**Example:** "You need an IEC to export goods or services from India. It is a 10-digit code issued by the DGFT. Without it, your shipments do not clear customs."

---

### 05. State the Consequence, Not Just the Service

Compliance has real consequences for non-compliance. Use them. Not to scare — to educate.

Telling a reader what happens if they do not act is the fastest way to make them understand why they should.

**Example:** "Professional Tax registration is mandatory for all employers in applicable states. Missing the deadline attracts penalties and potential legal notices."

---

### 06. Numbers Make Statements Trustworthy

Vague claims feel like marketing. Specific numbers feel like facts.

Use numbers wherever they exist — timelines, penalties, fees, thresholds, deadlines. If a specific number is not available, use a range. Never leave a claim unsupported when a number can support it.

**Wrong:** "GST registration takes a few days."

**Right:** "GST registration typically takes 7 to 10 working days from document submission."

---

### 07. The Reader Is Always "You"

Every page speaks directly to one person. Not "businesses," not "clients," not "entities."

Use "you" and "your" throughout. This is not casual — it is direct. B2B buyers are people making decisions. Speak to the person, not the company.

**Wrong:** "Businesses are required to file annual ROC returns."

**Right:** "You must file annual returns with the ROC every year. Missing this attracts a penalty of ₹100 per day."

---

### 08. Structure Pages Like a Decision Journey

Every service page follows this order:

1. **What it is** — one sentence, plain English
2. **Who needs it** — specific, not generic
3. **What happens if you don't have it** — consequence
4. **What we do** — process in 3 to 5 steps
5. **What you need to get started** — documents or information required
6. **CTA** — one clear next action

This structure means a reader who spends 60 seconds on a page knows exactly what to do next.

---

### 09. Headlines Are Statements, Not Labels

Labels describe. Statements communicate.

A headline label says what a section is. A headline statement tells the reader something they need to know.

**Label:** "Our GST Services"

**Statement:** "GST done right means zero notices and full ITC claims."

Every H1, H2, and H3 on the site should be a statement the reader can act on or learn from.

---

### 10. CTAs Tell the Reader the Next Step, Not a Vague Action

"Contact Us" tells the reader nothing. "Get started" tells the reader nothing.

Every CTA on this site names the specific next step.

**Use:**
- "Talk to a filing expert"
- "Tell us about your business"
- "Get your company registered"
- "Check your GST compliance"

Never use: Contact Us, Learn More, Click Here, Submit, Get Started.

---

### 11. Trust Is Built Through Specificity, Not Claims

Never write "we are trusted" or "we are experts" or "we provide the best service."

Trust is earned through specific, verifiable statements. Show the number of filings completed. Show the years in operation. Show the types of businesses served. Show client outcomes.

If a claim cannot be backed by a specific fact, cut it.

---

### 12. Tone: Confident, Plain, Direct

The voice of RequiredFilings is a knowledgeable friend — not a salesperson, not a lawyer, not a bureaucrat.

This friend knows compliance deeply. They explain it simply. They tell you exactly what to do. They do not hedge, they do not oversell, and they do not use language to sound impressive.

**Tone in three words: Clear. Direct. Helpful.**

---

## What This Site Never Does

- Uses jargon without explanation
- Writes a sentence longer than 12 words
- Opens a paragraph with "we"
- Makes a claim without a number or fact to back it
- Uses: leverage, utilize, facilitate, comprehensive, end-to-end, seamless, robust, holistic, one-stop, solutions, empower
- Writes passive voice ("returns are required to be filed" → "you must file returns")
- Buries the outcome at the bottom of the page

---

## The Premium Content Test

Before any page goes live, ask these five questions:

1. Can a business owner with zero compliance knowledge read this and understand it in under 90 seconds?
2. Does every section open with what the reader gets, not what we do?
3. Is every claim backed by a number, a fact, or a consequence?
4. Does the page have one clear CTA and one clear next step?
5. Does this read like a knowledgeable friend talking, or a legal document?

If the answer to any of these is no, the page is not ready.

---

## Quick Reference Card

| Do | Do Not |
|---|---|
| Lead with outcomes | Lead with service names |
| Use "you" and "your" | Use "clients" or "businesses" |
| State consequences | Assume readers understand stakes |
| Use specific numbers | Use vague qualifiers |
| Write 8–12 word sentences | Write compound sentences |
| Explain every acronym on first use | Assume readers know the terminology |
| Make headlines statements | Make headlines labels |
| Name the next step in every CTA | Use generic CTAs |
| Let specifics build trust | Claim trustworthiness directly |
